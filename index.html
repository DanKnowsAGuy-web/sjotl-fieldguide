Beginning the Journey

An inquirer is someone beginning to explore the Orthodox Christian faith and life. At St. John of the Ladder, inquiry is not treated as a mere academic program or membership process. It is the beginning of a relationship with Christ, His Church, and a concrete parish community.

People arrive at the Church from many different places and for many different reasons. Some come after years of searching. Others come with very little background in Christianity at all. Some arrive carrying confusion, wounds, bad experiences, internet theology, or unrealistic expectations.

To all of this we first say: take your time. The Church is interested in stability, honesty, patience, humility, and long-term faithfulness far more than speed.

The Orthodox Church is learned through worship, prayer, repentance, participation in parish life, and spiritual formation under the guidance of the Church. Books, podcasts, and online resources can support this process when they remain grounded in the concrete life of the parish and the guidance of the clergy. For many modern people, this remains a hurdle and a temptation.

During this period, inquirers are encouraged to begin establishing a consistent rhythm of Orthodox Christian life in conversation with a priest.

This ordinarily includes:

  • Learning the basic teachings and practices of the Orthodox faith
  • Attending the divine services regularly
  • Developing stability and consistency in one’s spiritual life
  • Becoming integrated into the life of the parish
  • Learning to pray and to fast
  • Building trust and openness with the clergy
  • Growing in humility, patience, and obedience

Inquiry is a gradual entrance into the life of the Church. The goal is not sprinting to reception into Orthodoxy, but the formation of a faithful Orthodox Christian life rooted in Christ, the sacraments, prayer, and the parish community. We have in mind your long-term spiritual health.

During Inquiry

While every person’s journey is unique, inquirers at our parish are generally expected to:

Attend Services Consistently

Regular attendance at the Sunday Divine Liturgy is essential. Inquirers are also encouraged to attend Vespers, feast days, and other services as they are able. This includes special services like marriages and funerals. You want as much exposure to the liturgical life of the Church as possible if it is to be your life.

Orthodoxy is learned primarily by praying, worshipping, and living within the liturgical life of the Church. The heart of the Christian life is the experience of God. For Orthodox Christians, the context of that is worship.

Attend Inquirer Gatherings

These sessions are held once a month and provide an opportunity for questions, discussion, and pastoral guidance in a smaller setting. It is an opportunity for newcomers to meet a priest and ask whatever is on their mind and their heart. This a setting to not only hear directly from a priest but also to ask questions that you are curious about or address any hangups or misconceptions you may have about Orthodoxy. We require that someone attends some services and one of these inquirer sessions before meeting with a priest one-on-one.

Attend Catechism Classes (what we call Metanoia)

Metanoia, often translated repentance, is a Greek word denoting a total transformation of mind. Catechism classes provide structured formation in the teachings, worship, history, and spiritual life of the Church. Inquirers should begin attending Metanoia as soon as they can if they think they have the intention of eventually joining the Church. There are two “semesters” of Metanoia, fall (starting end of August) and spring (starting beginning of Jan). Another component of Metanoia are Praxis Classes, which occur on some Sundays following Divine Liturgy.

Become Part of Parish Life

Orthodoxy is communal. Inquirers should seek to know the people of the parish, participate in meals and events (especially consider joining a Hospitality Team), and begin forming healthy spiritual friendships.

Over time, inquiry should lead toward rootedness and stability within the parish community rather than remaining perpetually at a distance.

Develop a Rule of Prayer and Fasting

With the guidance of the clergy, inquirers should begin developing a simple, consistent rule of prayer and fasting appropriate to their circumstances and level of spiritual maturity. Neither of these should be undertaken on your own and developed ad-hoc.

Orthodox spiritual disciplines are learned gradually and practiced within the guidance of the Church rather than undertaken independently.

Practice Stability

The Church looks for consistency, humility, patience, and seriousness of intention over time. We will address pitfalls below, but the central one for most modern people is simple consistency and patience, rooted in place. Orthodoxy is concerned with hesychia: the cultivation of stillness and silence.

The Role of the Clergy in Your Journey

The priest is a spiritual father and pastor entrusted with the care of souls. While he often must serve many roles—administrator, teacher, manager, and so on—his fundamental role is to help you to experience God through the mysterious (sacramental) life of Christ’s Church, which includes elucidating the doctrinal and moral teachings that flow from it.

In these respects, the priest serves as an extension of the office and ministry of the bishop, who stands in apostolic succession and has been entrusted by Christ and the Church with the authority to preach the Gospel, shepherd the faithful, bind and loose sins, and rightly divide the word of truth.

The clergy are therefore charged by the Church with:

  • Preaching and teaching the Orthodox faith faithfully
  • Guiding spiritual formation
  • Helping discern readiness for catechumenate and reception
  • Providing pastoral counsel, discernment, and correction
  • Protecting inquirers and catechumens from confusion and spiritual instability
  • Helping individuals learn to live an Orthodox life within the mind of the Church

For this reason, inquirers and catechumens should be intentionally developing a relationship with the clergy.

Questions regarding theology, spiritual life, relationships, moral struggles, major life decisions, and discernment should ultimately be directed to the priest rather than handled independently or outsourced primarily to peers or the internet.

In short, the Orthodox spiritual life is learned within the concrete life of the Church and under the pastoral care of her clergy.

The Role of the Laity

The parish community plays an essential role in receiving and supporting inquirers and catechumens. We are giving to one another to grow in love and patience. The parish is full of people who are on the same journey as you are, even if further along. One should have appropriate expectations that people sometimes fail, but also that other people are our salvation since they ground us, hold us accountable, and challenge us to grow in virtue.

Faithful parishioners can:

  • Model an Orthodox life
  • Offer friendship and hospitality
  • Encourage consistency and patience
  • Help newcomers become integrated into parish life
  • Pray for and support those entering the Church

Faithful Orthodox Christians help embody the life of the Church in concrete and practical ways. Their friendship, example, encouragement, and steadiness are often indispensable in helping newcomers become rooted in parish life. It is normal and encouraged to seek out the experience of the “Old Guard,” those who have been living the Orthodox faithfully for many years. At the same time, spiritual guidance and pastoral oversight belong properly to the clergy.

Sponsors & Spiritual Friendships

As inquirers and catechumens become rooted in parish life, they will often begin forming deeper spiritual friendships within the community.

One important relationship for formally becoming a catechumen is that of a sponsor or godparent.

A sponsor should be a faithful Orthodox Christian who demonstrates maturity, steadiness, sobriety, consistency in the life of the Church, and genuine care for the person entering Orthodoxy.

Ideally, a sponsor is someone with whom a natural relationship has developed over time through participation in parish life. Choosing a sponsor is therefore connected to becoming genuinely integrated into the community.

At the same time, there should be no anxiety or pressure surrounding this process. A sponsor need not be your closest confidant, but simply another touchpoint in the parish. Someone you can ask questions of and someone who will hold you accountable and support you in your spiritual journey.

The role of a sponsor is to:

  • Pray for and encourage the catechumen
  • Help them become rooted in parish life
  • Model a faithful Orthodox life
  • Offer friendship, support, and practical guidance
  • Continue supporting them after reception into the Church

Healthy spiritual friendships are marked by humility, steadiness, mutual respect, and a shared commitment to Christ and the life of the Church. You should not wait until the last minute to find a sponsor. The clergy cannot serve as your sponsors, nor can the clergy select them for you. Per above, finding a sponsor is an integral part of the process.

Entering the Catechumenate

At some point, inquiry must give way to commitment and stability. The catechumenate marks the beginning of that deeper commitment to the life of the Church.

Catechumens are those formally preparing to enter the Orthodox Church through baptism and/or chrismation. The analogy is often engagement to be married. You have decided you want to wed yourself to Christ and His Church.

At our parish, individuals are generally invited to become catechumens after:

  • Completing a semester of catechism
  • Demonstrating regular attendance at services
  • Showing stability and commitment
  • Becoming integrated into parish life
  • Developing a basic prayer life, fasting discipline, and relationship with the clergy
  • Demonstrating a sincere desire to conform one’s life to the teachings of the Church
  • Serving on a Hospitality Meal Team
  • Beginning to give of your treasure (e.g. tithe) as you are able

Because entering the catechumenate is a public step toward reception into the Orthodox Church, individuals should not be actively remaining in ways of life fundamentally incompatible with Orthodox Christian faith and practice.

This may include ongoing patterns such as:

  • Addictive or destructive behaviors without any effort toward repentance or healing
  • Sexual or romantic relationships contrary to the teaching of the Church
  • Participation in occult or esoteric practices
  • Membership in secret, anti-Christian, or spiritually incompatible organizations or societies
  • Other serious patterns of life incompatible with Christian discipleship

The Church does not expect perfection. Every Christian struggles with sin and weakness. What is sought is honesty, repentance, humility, and a genuine commitment to spiritual transformation.

For this reason, catechumens are expected to be open and truthful with the clergy regarding serious spiritual, moral, relational, or legal matters that may affect their spiritual life or preparation for entering the Church. Past sins, including those that may have resulted in legal consequences, should be disclosed to the clergy so that appropriate pastoral guidance, discernment, and care may be provided.

At St. John of the Ladder, catechumens are normally enrolled at appointed times during the year, especially:

  • Theophany (Jan 6)
  • Pentecost

Reception into the Orthodox Church likewise normally takes place at appointed times during the liturgical year, especially:

  • Holy Saturday before Pascha
  • The Eve of Nativity (Dec 24)

Once formally received as catechumens, individuals should come forward during the Divine Liturgy for the Prayer of the Catechumens and then return to their place afterward. This practice belongs specifically to catechumens and is not done by inquirers.

Life as a Catechumen

Catechumens are expected to continue and deepen the practices begun during inquiry:

  • Faithful attendance at services
  • Continued catechetical formation
  • Stable and consistent prayer rule
  • Increasing participation in the fasting and liturgical life of the Church
  • Spiritual honesty with a priest
  • Obedience to pastoral guidance
  • Continued integration into the parish community
  • Beginning to prayerfully discern a patron saint

Catechumens should begin developing a greater sense of responsibility and accountability within the life of the Church. This is a stage of increasing stability and belonging.

Stability, Parish Life, & Visiting Other Churches

Inquirers are welcome to visit other Orthodox parishes while they are learning about the life of the Church and discerning where they will ultimately put down roots. At the same time, healthy spiritual formation eventually requires stability, rootedness, and commitment to a concrete parish community.

Over time, inquirers should prayerfully discern whether they are prepared to remain and be formed within the life of St. John of the Ladder.

Once someone becomes a catechumen, they are understood to be under the pastoral care and spiritual oversight of this parish and its clergy. Catechumens should therefore not engage in “church hopping” or move freely between parishes without guidance.

If catechumens need or desire to visit another parish — whether locally or while traveling — they should first receive the blessing of the priest.

It is also good Orthodox etiquette to contact the priest of the parish being visited beforehand, introducing oneself, identifying one’s home parish and priest, and explaining when and why one will be visiting.

This practice helps cultivate humility, accountability, ecclesial order, and genuine rootedness within the life of the Church. The goal is not control or isolation, but stability, clarity, and real spiritual formation within a concrete community.

Choosing a Patron Saint

Catechumens preparing for reception into the Church should begin thinking prayerfully about a patron saint. Whenever possible, individuals are encouraged to retain their given name if it is already the name of a recognized Christian saint.

If a new name is needed, a patron saint should generally be chosen based on:

  • A genuine spiritual connection or admiration
  • Similarity of name where appropriate
  • The saint’s life, virtues, or witness
  • A relationship developed through prayer and reading

A patron saint is not chosen as a matter of aesthetics, novelty, or personal branding, but as a heavenly intercessor and model of Christian life. Questions regarding patron saints should be discussed with the clergy.

Some Common Temptations & Difficulties

Staying Embodied

The internet can be a useful supplement for learning, especially when used with discernment and under pastoral guidance. At the same time, genuine Orthodox formation takes place principally through the lived experience of the Church. The internet can also be a place that excites the passions, cultivates outrage, hated, and self-righteousness, and creates caricatures of reality.

Growth in Orthodoxy is nourished through:

  • The liturgical life of the Church
  • The Scriptures
  • The clergy
  • The saints
  • The sacramental and prayer life
  • The concrete relationships and rhythms of parish life

Online voices should always remain secondary to one’s actual life in the Church and relationship with the parish clergy. To be at St John of the Ladder and join the Orthodox Church is to give yourself to this place and its people.

Inquirers and catechumens are not permitted to teach or comment online about the Orthodox Faith. We often encourage that one’s journey into Orthodoxy should actually entail a sober analysis and evaluation of one’s relationship to online content, screens, and social media.

Learning Slowly and Humbly

A serious intellectual interest in Orthodoxy can be a genuine gift and often reflects a sincere desire for truth. We never want to indulge a caricature that the intellectual life and its skills are useless or should be avoided.

At the same time, Orthodox formation is ultimately about learning a way of life shaped by prayer, repentance, worship, humility, and participation in the Church. There are genuine dangers to over-intellectualizing and getting “lost in your head.” The Church recognizes these dangers and wants to guide the flock accordingly.

Reading, study, and theological discussion are healthiest when they deepen:

  • Prayer
  • Repentance
  • Humility
  • Patience
  • Trust in the life of the Church
  • Love for Christ and neighbor

Knowledge bears good fruit when it serves spiritual growth rather than argumentation, anxiety, or self-assertion. The life of the mind should serve action and inspire the work necessary to cultivate the habits and dispositions of virtuous living and keeping God’s commandments.

Seeking Mature Formation

Friendships among inquirers and catechumens can be a source of encouragement and mutual support. At the same time, as indicated above, lasting formation in the Orthodox life comes through the guidance of the clergy, the example of mature Orthodox Christians, and long-term immersion in the worship and rhythms of the Church.

Healthy growth is strengthened by relationships marked by stability, sobriety, humility, patience, and faithfulness rather than constant speculation, ideological enthusiasm, or debate.

For this reason, inquirers and catechumens should refrain from presenting themselves publicly as teachers or representatives of the Orthodox faith online or elsewhere during their formation. Likewise, one should be cautious of being formed by peers, other newcomers, and those who themselves have only recently been received into the Orthodox Church.

This season is meant to cultivate listening, learning, prayer, and spiritual rootedness within the life of the Church.

Growing Patiently, Tempering Zeal

Orthodox spiritual life develops gradually over time. Nothing worth having is easy, and no difficulty is overcome overnight. The temptation for newcomers is to be enamored with everything that is new and novel. Many people, having come from backgrounds that seemed to lack spiritual depth, discipline, and accountability, are quickly inspired by the substance of rigor of Orthodox life.

But what is not so easily accessible is the rich spiritual discernment imbedded in the Orthodox Church, which understand that people are different, with different circumstances and gifts, and different capacities.

Trying to attend every single service, doing lots of prayers at home, diving into the Jesus Prayer, engaging in rigorous fasting, obsessing over the “right” way to do everything, is a mark not of spiritual maturity or rigor but misplaced zeal, inexperience, and naivete.

Long-term spiritual growth is nourished by humility, consistency, patience, and endurance rather than intensity alone. Your spiritual life must last a lifetime. It is important to not be overcome by emotion and novelty, but to get serious about the real work that needs to be done. As the Aesop fable goes, slow and steady wins the race.

Romantic Relationships & Spiritual Stability

Inquiry and catechumenate are periods of major spiritual and personal formation. But many people enter the Orthodox Church due to a crisis of loneliness and meaninglessness and especially hoping for stable and “traditional” romantic relationships.

However, romantic relationships can introduce significant emotional complexity and shifting priorities during an already important season of discernment and formation. It is generally healthiest to focus first on becoming spiritually grounded, developing stability, establishing a relationship with the parish and clergy, and learning the rhythms of Orthodox life.

It is not permissible for existing members to romantically engage newcomers, to court inquirers, and to petition catechumens for a relationship. This activity poses a considerable risk to someone trying to put Christ first and join the Church.

Questions regarding relationships and dating should therefore be approached thoughtfully and discussed openly with the clergy.

A Final Encouragement

Orthodoxy is not a hobby, aesthetic, ideology, or intellectual system. It is the life of Christ in His Church. As has been repeated frequently here, the path into the Church requires patience, humility, repentance, faithfulness, and trust. You are looking for a transformation of life, of encounter with the Lord Jesus, and not simply another set of abstract beliefs or social commitments.

Do not rush. Be present. Attend the services consistently. Pray every day, even when it feels difficult or dry. Stay close to the clergy and the life of the parish. Listen more than you speak. Learn to love Christ, His saints, and His people.

And remember that the goal is not simply to become Orthodox, but to become holy.

We are glad you are here, and we are grateful to walk this path with you. May God bless you.
